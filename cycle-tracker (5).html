Your cycle. Your conditions. Your rules.

Days 1–5 · Menstrual Phase

Rest. Bleed. Survive.

Your body is doing heavy work. Energy dips, pain may peak on Days 1–2. This is not the time to push — it's the time to protect your reserves and adapt your environment to your needs.

What to Prioritize

🛏️Maximize rest — short naps are fine
💊Take pain relief early — don't wait for it to peak
🚫Postpone major decisions or confrontations
🍵Warm food, warm liquids, warm compress
📵Reduce social obligations — protect your energy

Travel Difficulty

🔴 High Difficulty
  • Carry double the supplies you think you need
  • Book aisle seats for bathroom access
  • Map bathrooms at transit points in advance
  • Pack a small heating patch for cramps
  • Avoid long overnight travel on Day 1–2 if possible
  • Wear dark, comfortable, loose clothing

Alternate-Day Guidance

Day 1 — The Crash

Flow starts. Cramps may be at their worst. Prostaglandins peak, causing pain and sometimes nausea or loose stools.

  • Take ibuprofen/mefenamic acid before pain spikes if possible
  • Eat something light before meds — avoid empty stomach
  • Cancel non-essential plans without guilt
  • Low activity only: walking around the house is enough

Day 2 — Peak Flow

Typically the heaviest flow day. Energy is still low but pain may begin to ease slightly toward evening.

  • Change products more frequently — don't wait for leaks
  • Iron-rich foods: lentils, spinach, jaggery, red meat if accessible
  • Stay hydrated — bleeding increases fluid loss
  • Light stretching or yoga if tolerable; no intense cardio

Day 3 — Turning Point

Flow begins to lighten for most. A small energy uptick may appear. The fog lifts slightly.

  • This is a good day to tackle one small, manageable task
  • Gentle walks outside can help mood
  • Start reintroducing social contact if you want it
  • Reflect or journal — your mind is more receptive now

Day 4 — Recovery Mode

Light flow or spotting. Energy slowly returning. This is a transition day — don't overcommit yet.

  • You may feel better — resist the urge to overdo it
  • Plan your next week while in a calmer headspace
  • Light exercise is now well-tolerated
  • Focus on rebuilding nutrition — eat well today

Day 5 — Emerging

For most, flow ends or nearly ends. Estrogen begins to rise. Energy and motivation start climbing.

  • Great day to make plans for the upcoming follicular phase
  • Start light workouts — body is ready
  • Social energy is returning — reconnect if you pulled away
  • Mood generally lifts significantly today

Rise. Plan. Begin.

Estrogen climbs steadily. Your energy, optimism, and mental clarity improve day by day. This is your window for starting things — projects, conversations, adventures. Use it intentionally.

Day 6 — Fresh Start

The menstrual phase is behind you. Estrogen is rising and you'll likely notice improved mood and lighter feelings.

  • Do a gentle reset: tidy space, plan the week
  • Restock your nutrition after period losses
  • Start any creative or professional project you've been putting off

Day 8 — Building Momentum

Energy is noticeably better. Skin often clears. You may feel more confident and articulate.

  • Great day for difficult conversations or negotiations
  • Increase workout intensity — body handles it well
  • Good day to reach out to people you've been avoiding

Day 10 — Peak Follicular

Near-peak estrogen. You likely feel your most focused and socially switched-on.

  • Book important presentations or interviews for this window
  • Social energy is high — great for group activities

Day 12 — Pre-Ovulation

Estrogen at or near its peak. LH surge beginning. You may notice increased libido and confidence.

  • Capture your best work — write, create, produce
  • If tracking fertility, this window matters
  • Transition mindset: ovulation approaches, pace yourself

  • Don't over-schedule the coming week — you'll thank yourself

Progesterone dominates. Energy gradually declines. Detail-orientation and nesting instincts increase. PMS symptoms may appear in Days 24–28. This phase rewards structure, completion, and self-compassion.

Day 24 — PMS Window Begins

Progesterone starts dropping if no pregnancy. For many, this is when PMS symptoms emerge or worsen.

  • Be extra patient with yourself and others
  • Avoid scheduling high-stakes situations if possible
  • Magnesium, B6, and complex carbs can ease symptoms
  • Track symptoms to understand your patterns over time

Day 27 — Pre-Menstrual

Final days before menstruation. Hormones drop sharply. Cramping, mood dips, and fatigue often peak now.

  • Prepare your period supplies now — don't get caught off-guard
  • Rest as much as possible — tomorrow's phase needs your reserves
  • Comfort, warmth, and low-demand activities only
  • It's okay to feel this hard — it's hormonal, not permanent

Long Train Journeys

Hours on a train can feel very different depending on your cycle phase. Overnight trains, limited bathrooms, and rigid seating demand specific planning — especially on heavy flow days.

🔴 Menstrual: Plan heavily 🟠 Luteal: Moderate prep 🟢 Follicular/Ovulation: Manageable

🚿 Hygiene Strategies

  • Bring a zip pouch with supplies — don't dig through bags in tiny bathrooms
  • Period underwear as a backup layer for overnight trains
  • Carry biodegradable wet wipes for quick freshening up
  • Change products before boarding, not mid-journey when possible
  • Pack a small spray deodorant — trains get warm and sweaty
  • Keep one clean outfit accessible in your bag, not buried

🚻 Bathroom Planning

  • Book seats near the toilet — check the train map when booking
  • Go at stations — train bathrooms are often locked or unusable mid-journey
  • Note which car has the cleanest toilet early in the journey
  • Use a menstrual cup on very long routes to reduce bathroom trips
  • Carry a small bag to dispose of products — bins are often missing

💊 Pain Management

  • Take pain meds 30 min before boarding if Day 1–2
  • Stick-on heat patches (Moov, ThermaCare) are ideal — hands-free
  • Book a window seat — leaning helps; aisle gets bumped constantly
  • Walk the aisle every 1–2 hours — movement eases cramping
  • Avoid cold drinks — warm water or herbal tea if available

😴 Sleep Hacks

  • Bring an eye mask and earplugs — non-negotiable on overnight trains
  • A small travel pillow for your lower back reduces cramp discomfort
  • Book a lower berth if possible — climbing is harder on heavy flow days
  • Wear loose pants or a comfortable wrap — avoid waistbands that press
  • Keep pain meds accessible at night — don't wait for pain to wake you fully

🔒 Safety Tips

  • Keep your bag close when going to the toilet — don't leave valuables exposed
  • Tell a travel companion your phase if you need more bathroom/stop time
  • Avoid sleeping on top berths alone in mixed compartments if uncomfortable
  • Trust your gut — discomfort from cramps vs. unsafe situations feel different
  • Have your accommodation address saved offline before boarding

Bus Travel

Bus travel is often the most budget-accessible but the most physically uncomfortable option — bumpy roads, no guaranteed bathroom stops, cramped seats. Preparation makes it bearable.

🔴 Menstrual: Hardest mode 🟠 Luteal: Challenging 🟢 Follicular: Most tolerable

🚿 Hygiene Strategies

  • Use your highest-capacity product before boarding — no mid-ride changes
  • Double up: tampon or cup + period underwear as backup
  • Bring sealed bags for used products — bus stops rarely have bins
  • Hand sanitizer is essential — bus stop bathrooms are often unsanitary
  • Compressed travel towels for quick wipe-downs at stops

🚻 Bathroom Planning

  • Ask driver or research: when and where are the scheduled stops?
  • Go at every stop regardless of urgency — next one may be 3+ hours away
  • Sit near the front — easier to ask for emergency stops
  • Reduce liquid intake 1 hour before boarding if bathroom access is uncertain
  • Carry small change for paid public toilets at stops

💊 Pain Management

  • Bumpy roads worsen cramps — time pain meds to last the journey
  • Sit near the middle of the bus — less road vibration than the back
  • Heat patches applied before boarding; they last 8–12 hours
  • Ginger chews or peppermint help with nausea from motion + cramps
  • Tense and release leg muscles to maintain circulation on long rides

😴 Sleep Hacks

  • Neck pillow is essential — bus seats offer no head support
  • Noise-cancelling earphones or foam plugs for engine noise
  • Bring a light blanket or shawl — overnight buses are often over-AC'd
  • Aisle seat for easier movement but window seat for leaning — choose based on phase
  • Avoid screens if prone to motion sickness; audiobooks or music instead

🔒 Safety Tips

  • Keep your period bag inside your main bag, not in overhead bins
  • Inform a co-traveler if you're feeling faint or unwell
  • On overnight buses, lock your bag to the seat frame if possible
  • Know the route and major stops — don't rely solely on driver announcements
  • If you feel unsafe, trust that feeling — ask to be dropped at a busy area

Flights & Jet Lag

Altitude, cabin pressure, time zone shifts, and recycled air all interact with your cycle. Jet lag can throw off your period entirely. Here's how to manage in the air and on landing.

🔴 Menstrual: High management 🟣 Luteal: Bloating + jet lag 🟢 Follicular: Easiest to fly

🚿 Hygiene Strategies

  • Cabin air is extremely dry — stay hydrated to prevent irritation and odor
  • Change products in the airport before boarding, not on the plane
  • Pack supplies in your personal item bag, not checked luggage
  • Menstrual cup or disc is ideal for flights — fewer changes needed
  • Unscented wipes for freshening up in tiny airplane bathrooms
  • Compression socks help circulation, especially on Day 1–2

🚻 Bathroom Planning

  • Book an aisle seat for bathroom access without climbing over people
  • Go right when the seatbelt sign turns off — lines build fast
  • Bring supplies in a small pouch in your pocket or front bag
  • Dispose in the bin inside the bathroom — do not flush pads or cups
  • On long-haul flights, plan to change every 4–5 hours minimum

💊 Pain Management

  • Altitude can worsen bloating — avoid carbonated drinks and salty snacks
  • Take ibuprofen before takeoff if you're in a pain-heavy phase
  • Heat patches work at altitude — apply before boarding
  • Walk the aisle every 1–2 hours — deep vein thrombosis risk rises during menstruation
  • Avoid alcohol — it worsens cramps, dehydration, and jet lag

😴 Sleep & Jet Lag

  • Jet lag can delay or advance your next period by 3–7 days — track this
  • Melatonin (0.5–1mg) helps reset circadian rhythm after crossing time zones
  • Sleep during the destination's nighttime, not departure city's
  • On landing, get natural sunlight immediately to anchor your new rhythm
  • Don't nap for more than 20 min on arrival day — push through to local bedtime
  • Luteal phase + jet lag = mood crash; give yourself grace and eat well

🔒 Safety Tips

  • Keep medications in carry-on — never check essential pain meds
  • Know the medical term for your condition if you have PCOS or endometriosis
  • Period products must be in your carry-on for security access
  • Inform cabin crew if you feel faint or unwell — they are trained to help
  • Have travel health insurance that covers gynecological emergencies

Backpacking

Backpacking means inconsistent access to bathrooms, pharmacies, and comfort. Weight limits, unpredictable terrain, and shared accommodation demand a lean but comprehensive period kit.

🔴 Menstrual Days 1–2: Hardest 🟠 Luteal: Plan for PMS flares 🟢 Follicular/Ovulation: Best for big days

🚿 Hygiene Strategies

  • Menstrual cup or disc: reusable, lightweight, no waste problem — best for backpacking
  • Period underwear as the only backup you need with a cup
  • Sterilize your cup with boiling water whenever possible; use Milton tablets when not
  • Bar soap for washing hands works better than liquid on a packed trip
  • Biodegradable wipes — use sparingly and pack out your waste
  • Quick-dry underwear dries overnight — two pairs is enough with daily washing

🚻 Bathroom Planning

  • Research bathroom access along your planned route before you leave
  • In remote areas: learn Leave No Trace period waste disposal rules
  • Carry a trowel for wilderness hygiene if camping
  • Use a pee cloth (Kula Cloth or similar) to reduce TP waste
  • Pack a small LED torch for nighttime bathroom trips at campsites

💊 Pain Management

  • Carry ibuprofen and a backup painkiller — pharmacies may be far
  • Physical activity from hiking actually reduces cramp intensity over time
  • On bad pain days: reduce pack weight, slow pace, don't hero through it
  • TENS devices (small ones) are surprisingly packable and effective
  • Magnesium supplementation daily reduces cramp severity over the cycle

😴 Sleep Hacks

  • Dark period underwear under your sleep layer — worry-free nights
  • Sleep on your side with knees slightly bent to ease cramp pressure
  • A small heat pack (chemical, single-use) in your sleeping bag for bad nights
  • Earplugs — hostels, campsites, and guesthouses are never fully quiet
  • Track your cycle in advance so you can plan tent vs. hostel nights accordingly

🔒 Safety Tips

  • Know where the nearest pharmacy or clinic is at each stop
  • Always carry a 3-day supply of period products beyond what you expect to need
  • Tell your travel buddy your cycle phase — they can help you on hard days
  • Don't attempt high-difficulty hikes on Day 1–2 if you have heavy flow
  • In areas with wildlife, store used products in sealed bags away from your sleeping area

Hostel vs Hotel

Shared bathrooms, mixed dorms, and thin walls are a different world from a private hotel room. Both are manageable — but need different approaches depending on your phase and needs.

🏨 Hostel Realities

  • Shared bathrooms mean you can't guarantee availability — always have backup products on you
  • Dorm settings: keep your period kit in a discreet but accessible pouch
  • Menstrual phase in a dorm = rough. Book a private room for Days 1–2 if budget allows
  • Communal fridges: don't store medication there — keep it with you
  • Night trips to shared bathrooms: wear slippers and keep a small torch handy
  • Other travelers are not your enemy — most people understand, but privacy still matters

🏩 Hotel Advantages

  • Private bathroom = significantly better for heavy flow days
  • Request extra towels at check-in — useful for comfort and warmth
  • Many hotels have hot water bottles on request — ask at the front desk
  • Room service or delivery saves you going out on bad pain days
  • If you leak on sheets, inform housekeeping early — they've seen it before and are trained for it
  • Use the "Do Not Disturb" sign aggressively on your rest days

💡 Hostel Survival Kit

  • Period pouch that clips to your wrist for bathroom runs
  • Small zip-lock bags for discrete disposal
  • Flip flops (mandatory for shared showers)
  • Mini padlock for your locker — keep products secured
  • Ear plugs + eye mask for sleeping in dorms during cramp-heavy nights

💡 Budget Hotel Hacks

  • Book refundable rooms around your expected menstrual days when possible
  • Dark towels/cloths from home for heavy nights — don't ruin hotel linens
  • Kettle in the room = hot water bottle with a fabric cover for cramp relief
  • Use hotel laundry or sink-wash period underwear overnight
  • Check if hotel has a pharmacy nearby before you arrive

😴 Sleep in Both Settings

  • Hostel: top bunk on heavy days = risky. Request bottom bunk at check-in
  • Hotel: ask for extra blankets — warmth helps cramps significantly
  • Both: travel sheet or sleep sack protects you and the bedding
  • Period underwear under pyjamas = peace of mind for uninterrupted sleep

Menstrual Phase — Food Guide

Iron loss is real. Inflammation is high. Your gut is sensitive. Eat warm, eat dense, eat often. This is not the time to eat light.

✓ Prefer

  • Lentil soups, dal, rajma — iron + protein
  • Jaggery in warm water or milk — iron without nausea
  • Bananas — potassium eases cramping muscles
  • Rice porridge / khichdi — easy on the gut
  • Ginger tea / warm lemon water — anti-inflammatory
  • Dark leafy greens (palak, methi) — replenish iron
  • Eggs — easily digestible protein
  • Oats with banana — slow carbs + magnesium

✗ Avoid

  • Fried foods — worsen prostaglandin inflammation
  • Salty chips / processed snacks — increase bloating
  • Caffeine — constricts blood vessels, worsens cramps
  • Cold drinks / ice cream — can intensify cramping
  • Refined sugar — energy spike then crash
  • Alcohol — dehydrates, worsens inflammation

→ Why It Helps

  • Iron-rich foods counter blood loss and fatigue
  • Magnesium (bananas, oats) relaxes uterine muscles
  • Warm foods keep the gut relaxed — cold triggers spasms
  • Ginger has proven anti-inflammatory action on period pain
  • Complex carbs sustain energy without blood sugar spikes

₹ Cheap Finds

  • Boiled egg from any stall (₹10–15)
  • Banana bunch (₹20–30 for 6)
  • Jaggery block (₹10) — dissolve in tea
  • Peanut packet (₹10) — magnesium + healthy fats

Follicular Phase — Food Guide

Estrogen rising. Your gut is resilient, metabolism active. Focus on building: protein, fermented foods, fresh vegetables. This is your easiest phase to eat well.

✓ Prefer

  • Fermented foods — curd, idli, dosa, kanji
  • Sprouts and legumes — high plant protein
  • Fresh vegetables and salads
  • Eggs, paneer, chicken — rebuild after period loss
  • Flaxseeds — phytoestrogens support estrogen rise
  • Green tea — mild caffeine OK now
  • Nuts and seeds — zinc and selenium

✗ Avoid

  • Excess sugar — disrupts hormonal rise
  • Ultra-processed snacks — kill gut flora rebuild
  • Skipping meals — your metabolism is high, feed it

→ Why It Helps

  • Fermented foods replenish gut bacteria lost during menstruation
  • Zinc supports follicle development and estrogen signaling
  • Flaxseeds bind excess estrogen metabolites
  • Higher protein intake supports muscle gains of this phase

₹ Cheap Finds

  • Curd / lassi (₹15–30) — cheap probiotic everywhere
  • Sprout cup (₹15) — easiest street protein
  • Egg bhurji + roti (₹30–50)
  • Moong dal chilla from roadside carts
  • Mixed nuts packet (₹20–30)
All Venues — Follicular Picks
  • Railway: Curd rice, masala chaas, dosa where available
  • Roadside: Sprout bhel, egg rolls, chana chaat, coconut water
  • Café: Green tea, egg sandwich, yogurt parfait
  • Bakery: Seeded bread, multigrain rolls, nut bars
  • Hostel: Scrambled eggs + canned beans + veg stir-fry
  • Buy eggs + lentils + curd for the week — covers the whole phase for ₹150–200

PCOS / PCOD

Polycystic ovary patterns often involve insulin resistance, androgen sensitivity, and irregular cycles. Most adaptations focus on blood sugar stability and inflammation reduction.

🥗 Diet Modifications

  • Eat every 3–4 hours — never skip meals, blood sugar is the priority
  • Low-glycaemic index foods: oats, legumes, sweet potato, brown rice
  • Pair every carb with protein or fat to slow glucose absorption
  • Anti-inflammatory foods: turmeric, ginger, omega-3 rich fish/flaxseed
  • Spearmint tea daily — shown to mildly reduce androgen levels
  • Fermented foods to support gut-hormone axis
  • Inositol-rich foods: citrus, beans, nuts, cantaloupe

🚫 Foods to Avoid

  • White bread, white rice, refined flour — rapid glucose spikes
  • Sugary drinks, packaged juices — worst insulin triggers
  • Dairy in excess — can increase androgen-related symptoms for some
  • Deep fried foods — worsen systemic inflammation
  • Alcohol — disrupts insulin sensitivity and liver detox of hormones
  • Artificial sweeteners — may worsen gut microbiome balance

✈️ Travel Warnings

  • Your period may arrive late or not at all — don't panic, carry supplies anyway
  • Jet lag and sleep disruption worsen insulin resistance — prioritise sleep
  • Airport and station food is often high-GI — carry your own snacks
  • Stress (travel-related) can delay ovulation further — build in rest days
  • Track symptoms rather than dates — cycle may shift significantly while travelling

⚡ Energy Management

  • Energy crashes are common — never go more than 4 hours without eating
  • Morning walk or light movement is more stabilising than intense workouts
  • Strength training 2–3x/week improves insulin sensitivity significantly
  • Avoid high-intensity exercise when you're exhausted — it raises cortisol
  • Power naps of 20 min are fine — longer naps can disrupt night sleep
  • Identify your 2–3 hour energy window and protect it for priority tasks

🌿 Natural Support

  • Spearmint tea (2 cups daily) — mild anti-androgen properties
  • Cinnamon in food — studied for improving insulin sensitivity
  • Seed cycling: flax + pumpkin in follicular, sesame + sunflower in luteal
  • Stress reduction is non-negotiable — cortisol directly worsens PCOS
  • Track your sleep — poor sleep is a major PCOS aggravator
  • Consistent mealtimes help regulate the hormonal rhythm

Irregular Cycles

Cycles outside the 21–35 day window, or those that vary widely month to month. Often linked to stress, sleep, under-eating, or hormonal transitions. Focus: regularity through lifestyle.

🥗 Diet Modifications

  • Eat enough calories — undereating is a primary driver of irregular cycles
  • Healthy fats are essential for hormone production: nuts, olive oil, avocado, coconut
  • Zinc-rich foods support ovulation: pumpkin seeds, chickpeas, eggs
  • Vitamin B6 and B12: support cycle regularity (chicken, eggs, bananas)
  • Regular mealtimes signal safety to the body — eat at consistent hours
  • Seed cycling as a gentle hormone-rhythm practice

🚫 Foods to Avoid

  • Crash dieting or skipping meals — tells your body reproduction is unsafe
  • Excess caffeine — elevates cortisol and disrupts the HPG axis
  • Alcohol — directly suppresses LH, which triggers ovulation
  • Very low-fat diets — hormones are made from fats; you need them
  • Ultra-processed food — disrupts gut microbiome that regulates estrogen

✈️ Travel Warnings

  • Always carry 7–10 days of supplies when travelling — never assume timing
  • Jet lag can delay your period by 5–14 days — keep tracking, not guessing
  • New time zones = new circadian rhythm = possible cycle shift
  • Track BBT or symptoms rather than calendar dates when abroad
  • High-stress travel periods may cause missed periods — rule out pregnancy first

⚡ Energy Management

  • Your energy won't follow a predictable pattern — don't plan around phases
  • Daily check-ins: how do you feel today? Adapt accordingly
  • Rest when you bleed, regardless of how unexpected the timing is
  • Build flexible work/travel structures — don't lock in intense plans too far ahead
  • Prioritise sleep consistently — it's the most powerful cycle regulator

🌿 Natural Support

  • Consistent sleep/wake times — the most underrated cycle regulator
  • Reduce high-intensity exercise if your cycle has recently become irregular
  • Seed cycling as a gentle rhythm tool across the month
  • Stress management practices: journaling, breathwork, or walks in nature
  • Ashwagandha (adaptogen) — may support stress-related cycle irregularity
  • Track symptoms in an app for 3+ months to spot your personal pattern

Painful Periods

Significant cramping, back pain, or nausea during menstruation. May be tied to prostaglandin levels, inflammation, or uterine sensitivity. Focus: reduce inflammation before and during.

🥗 Diet Modifications

  • Start anti-inflammatory eating 3–5 days before your expected period
  • Omega-3 rich foods: fish, flaxseed, walnuts — reduce prostaglandin intensity
  • Magnesium daily in the week before: pumpkin seeds, dark chocolate, oats
  • Ginger — as potent as ibuprofen in some studies for period pain
  • Turmeric with black pepper — curcumin reduces prostaglandin production
  • Warm, easily digestible meals on Day 1–2: khichdi, soup, porridge

🚫 Foods to Avoid

  • Fried and processed foods in the luteal phase — spike prostaglandins
  • Red meat (excess) — arachidonic acid raises inflammatory prostaglandins
  • Caffeine during period — constricts blood vessels, worsens cramping
  • Cold food and drinks on Day 1–2 — triggers muscle contractions
  • Salt — causes water retention that increases bloating and pressure
  • Alcohol — dehydrates and worsens pain perception

✈️ Travel Warnings

  • Never board a long journey on Day 1 without pain meds — take them preemptively
  • Stick-on heat patches are your most important travel item during period
  • Book aisle seats and flexible tickets around your expected Day 1–2
  • Research pharmacy availability at your destination before arriving
  • Carry your preferred painkiller brand — formulations vary by country
  • If pain is severe enough to affect walking, that day is a rest day — non-negotiable

⚡ Energy Management

  • Pain is exhausting — budget double the rest on Days 1–2
  • Don't push through severe pain — it's not strength, it's harm
  • Gentle movement (walking, stretching) is okay if pain is mild — not when severe
  • Eat small, frequent meals — nausea can make eating difficult
  • Warmth management: warm room, warm clothes, warm compress = lower pain
  • Pre-period: build up sleep and reduce load in the 3 days before expected start

🌿 Natural Support

  • Heat therapy — 40°C heat applied locally rivals ibuprofen in pain studies
  • Ginger tea (fresh grated) 3x daily from Day 1
  • Magnesium glycinate supplement from mid-luteal phase onward
  • Castor oil pack on the lower abdomen the week before — may reduce inflammation
  • Gentle yoga: child's pose, supine twist, cat-cow — specifically for cramps
  • TENS device (portable) — drug-free pain relief increasingly used for period pain

Heavy Bleeding

Soaking through a pad or tampon every 1–2 hours, or bleeding for more than 7 days. Heavy flow significantly increases iron loss and fatigue. Focus: replenishment, protection, pacing.

🥗 Diet Modifications

  • Iron-rich foods every single day during your period: dal, palak, jaggery, red meat
  • Pair iron with Vitamin C for absorption: lime with dal, tomato with spinach
  • Vitamin K foods: leafy greens, broccoli — supports blood clotting
  • Stay aggressively hydrated — heavy bleeding increases fluid loss
  • Protein at every meal — your body is losing protein through blood
  • Don't skip meals even when nauseous — blood sugar crashes worsen fatigue

🚫 Foods to Avoid

  • Alcohol — acts as a blood thinner, can worsen bleeding volume
  • Excess omega-6 (vegetable oils, processed snacks) — pro-inflammatory
  • Caffeine — constricts vessels but increases bleeding in some individuals
  • Very spicy food on heavy days — can irritate the gut alongside uterine activity
  • High sugar — worsens fatigue crashes during heavy blood loss

✈️ Travel Warnings

  • Double your product supply — always carry twice what you calculate needing
  • Menstrual disc or cup with high capacity are significantly better for heavy flow travel
  • Dark clothing on heavy days — functional, not fashionable, priority
  • Know where the closest bathroom is at all times — map it at each stop
  • Overnight transit on heavy flow days is high-risk — have a backup plan
  • If you feel faint or dizzy while travelling, stop immediately and eat + hydrate

⚡ Energy Management

  • Heavy bleeding = real blood loss = real fatigue — it's not in your head
  • Day 1–2 are rest days, full stop. Schedule nothing you can't cancel
  • Iron loss peaks on Day 2 — expect to feel worst around this time
  • Sit before you feel dizzy — stand up slowly from lying or sitting positions
  • Micro-rests every hour are more effective than one long rest later
  • Eat iron-rich food within the first hour of waking on heavy days

🌿 Natural Support

  • Raspberry leaf tea — traditionally used to tone uterine muscles and reduce heavy flow
  • Shepherd's purse (herb) — traditionally used to reduce excessive bleeding

  • Track your product usage per day — helps you understand your actual flow volume
  • If you're consistently soaking through hourly, please consult a doctor — this is one area where lifestyle is not enough alone

Anemia / Low Iron

Low hemoglobin from iron deficiency — common with heavy periods or low dietary iron intake. Symptoms include extreme fatigue, dizziness, brain fog, pale skin, breathlessness. Focus: constant replenishment.

🥗 Diet Modifications

  • Iron at every meal — this is not optional during your period
  • Heme iron (most absorbable): red meat, chicken liver, eggs
  • Non-heme iron (plant): lentils, rajma, chana, palak, jaggery, tofu
  • Always eat iron with Vitamin C: lime juice on dal, tomato with spinach
  • Jaggery is your cheapest, most portable iron source — 1 small piece daily
  • Iron-fortified foods: some flours, cereals — check labels when travelling
  • Cook in a cast iron pan — small amounts of iron leach into food

🚫 Foods to Avoid

  • Tea/coffee with meals — tannins block iron absorption by up to 60%
  • Calcium-rich foods at the same time as iron — calcium competes for absorption
  • Whole wheat bran in excess — phytates bind iron
  • Dairy with iron-rich meals — drink milk separately
  • High-oxalate foods (spinach in large amounts) — counterintuitive but oxalates bind iron

✈️ Travel Warnings

  • Dizziness is a real fainting risk — sit when you feel lightheaded, do not push through
  • Altitude travel worsens anemia symptoms — take it slower than usual
  • Long flights are exhausting for everyone, but brutal with anemia — build in recovery days
  • Always have a snack with iron on you — don't go hours without eating
  • Inform travel companions about your condition — they need to know if you collapse
  • Stairs, long walks with heavy bags — assess realistically. Ask for help.

⚡ Energy Management

  • Your fatigue is physiological, not motivational — iron deficiency literally starves your cells of oxygen
  • Plan the hardest tasks for mid-morning when possible — energy dips are sharper in afternoon
  • Short walks are better than no movement, but don't exhaust yourself
  • Sleep is curative — protect 8+ hours rigorously
  • Cold exposure worsens fatigue with anemia — stay warm
  • Track energy levels daily — you'll start to see when your iron is low before symptoms peak

🌿 Natural Support

  • Amla (Indian gooseberry) — highest natural Vitamin C source, dramatically boosts iron absorption
  • Jaggery + sesame laddoos — traditional iron + calcium combo that actually works
  • Moringa leaves (drumstick) — extremely high in iron, widely available in South Asia
  • Nettle tea — traditionally used to support iron levels
  • Cook with black sesame seeds — higher iron than white sesame
  • Beetroot juice — improves hemoglobin and blood oxygenation over time

Hormonal Acne

Breakouts that follow a cycle pattern — typically appearing around the jaw, chin, and cheeks in the luteal phase or during menstruation. Driven by androgens and inflammation. Focus: internal + topical.

🥗 Diet Modifications

  • Low-glycaemic diet is the single most impactful dietary change — cut white flour and sugar
  • Zinc daily: pumpkin seeds, chickpeas, eggs — zinc reduces sebum production
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: fish, flaxseed, walnuts — anti-inflammatory for skin
  • Probiotics: curd, kanji, fermented foods — gut-skin axis is real
  • Green tea (2 cups daily) — reduces androgens and inflammation
  • Colourful vegetables: antioxidants protect skin from internal inflammation
  • Water: hydration keeps skin cells functioning and toxins moving out

🚫 Foods to Avoid

  • Dairy — whey and casein stimulate IGF-1 which raises sebum and androgens
  • High-glycaemic foods: white rice, bread, sugar — spike insulin which spikes androgens
  • Chocolate (milk or white) — sugar + dairy combo is a breakout trigger
  • Whey protein supplements — directly linked to hormonal acne in multiple studies
  • Junk food and fast food — trans fats worsen inflammatory acne
  • Iodine-heavy foods in excess: seaweed, excess iodised salt

✈️ Travel Warnings

  • Aeroplane air is extremely drying — use a non-comedogenic moisturiser in flight
  • Don't touch your face — harder to remember when you're tired from travel
  • Change your pillowcase frequently in hostels and hotels — bacteria and oil buildup
  • Hard water in different cities can trigger breakouts — rinse with filtered water if possible
  • Travel stress = cortisol spike = acne flare — manage it proactively
  • Pack travel-size salicylic acid or niacinamide — don't start new products while travelling

⚡ Energy Management

  • Breakouts hit self-esteem — acknowledge the emotional drain, it's real
  • Luteal phase is the highest acne-risk window — reduce other stressors then
  • Sleep deprivation worsens hormonal acne directly — 8 hours is skincare
  • High-intensity exercise is good for hormones, but sweat left on skin is not — cleanse after
  • Tracking breakout timing vs cycle days helps predict and prepare, not panic

🌿 Natural Support

  • Spearmint tea (2 cups daily) — studied for reducing androgens linked to hormonal acne
  • Zinc supplement or zinc-rich foods — one of the most evidenced natural acne interventions
  • Raw honey as a spot treatment — antibacterial, anti-inflammatory
  • Turmeric face mask (with yogurt) — reduces inflammation topically
  • Seed cycling — flax + pumpkin in follicular may reduce luteal androgen peaks
  • Consistent sleep and stress management — not optional, foundational
