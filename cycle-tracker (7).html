Days 1–5 · Menstrual Phase

Rest. Bleed. Survive.

Your body is doing heavy work. Energy dips, pain may peak on Days 1–2. This is not the time to push — it's the time to protect your reserves and adapt your environment to your needs.

Travel Difficulty

🔴 High Difficulty
  • Carry double the supplies you think you need
  • Book aisle seats for bathroom access
  • Map bathrooms at transit points in advance
  • Pack a small heating patch for cramps
  • Avoid long overnight travel on Day 1–2 if possible
  • Wear dark, comfortable, loose clothing

Alternate-Day Guidance

Day 1 — The Crash

Flow starts. Cramps may be at their worst. Prostaglandins peak, causing pain and sometimes nausea or loose stools.

  • Take ibuprofen/mefenamic acid before pain spikes if possible
  • Eat something light before meds — avoid empty stomach
  • Cancel non-essential plans without guilt
  • Low activity only: walking around the house is enough

Day 2 — Peak Flow

Typically the heaviest flow day. Energy is still low but pain may begin to ease slightly toward evening.

  • Change products more frequently — don't wait for leaks
  • Iron-rich foods: lentils, spinach, jaggery, red meat if accessible
  • Stay hydrated — bleeding increases fluid loss
  • Light stretching or yoga if tolerable; no intense cardio

Day 3 — Turning Point

Flow begins to lighten for most. A small energy uptick may appear. The fog lifts slightly.

  • This is a good day to tackle one small, manageable task
  • Gentle walks outside can help mood
  • Start reintroducing social contact if you want it
  • Reflect or journal — your mind is more receptive now

Day 4 — Recovery Mode

Light flow or spotting. Energy slowly returning. This is a transition day — don't overcommit yet.

  • You may feel better — resist the urge to overdo it
  • Plan your next week while in a calmer headspace
  • Light exercise is now well-tolerated
  • Focus on rebuilding nutrition — eat well today

Day 5 — Emerging

For most, flow ends or nearly ends. Estrogen begins to rise. Energy and motivation start climbing.

  • Great day to make plans for the upcoming follicular phase
  • Start light workouts — body is ready
  • Social energy is returning — reconnect if you pulled away
  • Mood generally lifts significantly today

  • Start preparing for a slower pace ahead
  • Good day to organize, systematize, plan ahead
  • Don't over-schedule the coming week — you'll thank yourself
Days 17–28 · Luteal Phase

Slow. Complete. Inward.

Progesterone dominates. Energy gradually declines. Detail-orientation and nesting instincts increase. PMS symptoms may appear in Days 24–28. This phase rewards structure, completion, and self-compassion.

Alternate-Day Guidance

Day 17 — Early Luteal

Progesterone rising. Energy still decent. Mood may feel slightly calmer and more inward-focused.

  • Wrap up open loops from the ovulation phase
  • Good for administrative tasks, editing, and reviewing
  • Begin meal prepping if possible — cravings will increase

Day 19 — Settling In

Progesterone continues rising. You may prefer solitude or smaller groups over large social events.

  • Good for deep, focused work — fewer distractions tolerated
  • Start scaling back evening commitments
  • Yoga, walking, swimming over high-intensity training

Day 21 — Mid Luteal

Progesterone peaks. Some may notice breast tenderness, bloating, or increased appetite starting here.

  • Don't skip meals — blood sugar instability worsens PMS
  • Reduce caffeine and sodium if bloating is an issue
  • Notice emotional patterns — don't react to everything

Day 24 — PMS Window Begins

Progesterone starts dropping if no pregnancy. For many, this is when PMS symptoms emerge or worsen.

  • Be extra patient with yourself and others
  • Avoid scheduling high-stakes situations if possible
  • Magnesium, B6, and complex carbs can ease symptoms
  • Track symptoms to understand your patterns over time

Day 27 — Pre-Menstrual

Final days before menstruation. Hormones drop sharply. Cramping, mood dips, and fatigue often peak now.

  • Prepare your period supplies now — don't get caught off-guard
  • Rest as much as possible — tomorrow's phase needs your reserves
  • Comfort, warmth, and low-demand activities only
  • It's okay to feel this hard — it's hormonal, not permanent

Menstrual Phase — Food Guide

Iron loss is real. Inflammation is high. Your gut is sensitive. Eat warm, eat dense, eat often. This is not the time to eat light.

✓ Prefer

  • Lentil soups, dal, rajma — iron + protein
  • Jaggery in warm water or milk — iron without nausea
  • Bananas — potassium eases cramping muscles
  • Rice porridge / khichdi — easy on the gut
  • Ginger tea / warm lemon water — anti-inflammatory
  • Dark leafy greens (palak, methi) — replenish iron
  • Eggs — easily digestible protein
  • Oats with banana — slow carbs + magnesium

✗ Avoid

  • Fried foods — worsen prostaglandin inflammation
  • Salty chips / processed snacks — increase bloating
  • Caffeine — constricts blood vessels, worsens cramps
  • Cold drinks / ice cream — can intensify cramping
  • Refined sugar — energy spike then crash
  • Alcohol — dehydrates, worsens inflammation

→ Why It Helps

  • Iron-rich foods counter blood loss and fatigue
  • Magnesium (bananas, oats) relaxes uterine muscles
  • Warm foods keep the gut relaxed — cold triggers spasms
  • Ginger has proven anti-inflammatory action on period pain
  • Complex carbs sustain energy without blood sugar spikes

  • Boiled egg from any stall (₹10–15)
  • Banana bunch (₹20–30 for 6)
  • Jaggery block (₹10) — dissolve in tea
  • Peanut packet (₹10) — magnesium + healthy fats

🟡 When to Pause and Assess

  • Cramps severe enough to prevent walking or standing
  • Soaking through a pad or tampon every hour for 2+ hours
  • Feeling faint, dizzy, or lightheaded when standing
  • Nausea severe enough to prevent eating or drinking
  • Fever alongside period symptoms (unusual — take it seriously)
  • Pain that doesn't respond to your usual painkillers at correct dose
  • Bleeding that continues beyond 8–10 days without stopping

🔵 Travel-Safe Response Steps

  1. Stop moving. Find a place to sit or lie down immediately.
  2. Eat and hydrate. Low blood sugar worsens all symptoms — eat something, sip water slowly.
  3. Take pain relief if you have it and haven't already reached your safe dose.
  4. Apply heat if available — a heat patch, warm water bottle, or even warm water compress.
  5. Tell someone nearby — a companion, hotel staff, or fellow traveller — that you need a moment.
  6. Give it 20–30 minutes. Most acute symptoms ease with rest, warmth, and food.
  7. If symptoms don't ease or worsen — seek medical help. Don't wait it out alone.

🔴 Symptoms That Need Same-Day Medical Help

  • Soaking through products every 30 minutes or less
  • Passing clots larger than a 50-paise coin repeatedly
  • Fainting or nearly fainting — not just lightheadedness
  • Severe pain in one side of the lower abdomen (could indicate something other than period)
  • Fever above 38°C alongside pelvic pain
  • Difficulty breathing alongside heavy bleeding
  • Sudden change in flow pattern unlike anything you've experienced before

Travelling alone? Before every trip, share your itinerary with one trusted person who knows your cycle patterns. Set a daily check-in time. If you don't check in, they know to follow up. This is not paranoia — it's basic travel safety for anyone, amplified by the reality of travelling with a body that has monthly health variables.

This guide helps you tell the difference between normal period discomfort and symptoms that warrant attention. Most period experiences, even difficult ones, are not emergencies. But some signals are worth taking seriously. You know your body — trust it.

🩸

Heavy or Unusual Bleeding

  • Soaking a full pad or tampon in under an hour, two hours running
  • Passing clots consistently larger than a 10-rupee coin
  • Bleeding that lasts longer than 10 days without slowing
  • Bleeding between periods that isn't light spotting
  • Post-sex bleeding that recurs
  • Normal: heavier Day 1–2, lighter after that
😵

Dizziness & Fatigue

  • Fainting or blacking out — even briefly
  • Dizziness severe enough to prevent standing safely
  • Heart racing or pounding during your period
  • Extreme breathlessness with normal activity during period
  • Persistent fatigue that doesn't ease after rest and eating
  • Normal: tiredness on Days 1–2, improved by Day 3
🌡️

Pain Patterns

  • Pain that doesn't respond to standard painkillers at the correct dose
  • Sharp pain on one side only (not general cramping)
  • Pain that radiates to the shoulder or back without obvious cause
  • Pain during sex that gets worse over time
  • Pelvic pain that persists throughout the cycle, not just during period
  • Normal: cramping Days 1–2, easing by Day 3–4
📅

Cycle Abnormalities

  • No period for 3+ months without pregnancy or known cause
  • Cycle consistently shorter than 21 days or longer than 35
  • Sudden dramatic change in cycle length or flow volume
  • Periods that were regular and suddenly become very irregular
  • Spotting in the luteal phase that recurs every cycle
  • Normal: slight variation month-to-month (1–5 days)
🌡️

Other Warning Signs

  • Fever (38°C+) alongside pelvic pain or unusual discharge
  • Unusual discharge colour, smell, or texture alongside pain
  • Sudden swelling or bloating that doesn't ease after your period ends
  • Unexplained weight changes alongside cycle disruption
  • Hair loss or excessive facial hair appearing alongside irregular cycles
  • Normal: mild bloating in luteal, resolves with period
✅

What IS Normal

  • Cramping on Days 1–2, fading by Day 3
  • Heavier flow on Days 1–2, lighter by Day 4–5
  • Fatigue, mood changes, mild breast tenderness in luteal
  • Small clots (smaller than 10-rupee coin) occasionally
  • Cycle varying 1–5 days month to month
  • Bloating and appetite changes in the week before period

If You See a Red Flag While Travelling — Here's Your Plan

Step 1 — Don't dismiss itIf something feels different from your usual pattern, that's information. Trust your body's signals.
Step 2 — Stabilise firstSit, eat, hydrate. Take stock of all your symptoms calmly before deciding next steps.
Step 3 — Find a clinicSearch "gynaecologist near me" or go to the nearest hospital's outpatient department. You don't need a referral for walk-in.
Step 4 — Communicate clearlyTell the doctor: your cycle day, what's unusual, how long it's been happening. Be specific.
Step 5 — Have insurance readyTravel health insurance should cover gynaecological emergencies. Know your policy number and helpline before you leave.
Step 6 — Follow up at homeEven if the acute issue resolves, book a follow-up with your regular doctor when you're back.
